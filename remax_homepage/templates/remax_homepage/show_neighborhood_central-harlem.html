Central Harlem is bounded by Fifth Avenue on the East, Central Park on the South, Morningside Park, St. Nicholas Avenue and Edgecombe Avenue on the West, and the Harlem River on the North. Central Harlem is a neighborhood facing a new wave of social and economic gentrification, spurred by fierce crime fighting and retail development efforts.

Since its revolution of African American art and culture in the Harlem Renaissance, Central Harlem is abuzz with art and history. The culture of Central Harlem is very diverse, as there is a mix of the history of the neighborhood, as well as the waves of college students in the area. This neighborhood is become more and more popular due to the combination of affordable houses and great transportation, attracting a variety of styles.

As Manhattan’s real estate market again nears record-setting levels, Harlem finds itself in the heart of another real estate renaissance. New condominiums have replaced decades-old vacant lots, while stately yet neglected brownstones are being freshly renovated. With the real estate industry booming, there are plenty of condos, brownstones, and apartments at very reasonable prices, attracting major opportunities for investors worldwide.

Morris Jumel Museum

There are lot of major attractions in Central Harlem for you to have fun with during your free time.

For example, visit the Studio Museum, featuring a high caliber of arts, paintings, photos, and more by artists of African descent. You will definitely appreciate the culture and history of this region through the recognition of this artwork.

Or, try Morris-Jumel Mansion, which was built in 1765 by Colonel Roger Morris and his wife, Mary Philipse. This mansion is a landmarked historic site and museum, and Manhattan’s oldest house. Through historic site tours and education programs, the museum interprets the mansion in the context of domestic life in New York City from 1765 until 1865. You will be enamored by the complexity of the mansion and the unique historical perspective that it has to offer.

So, if you’re interested in learning the history of Central Harlem, there are many spots for you to visit and enjoy the culture.

Harlem River Park

With a variety of parks in the area, Central Harlem also provides marvelous public recreational space.

Visit the Harlem River Park, which was built by the city of New York along 20 acres of Harlem Waterfront. The park has restored the historic access by providing pedestrian access over Harlem River Drive, as well as supplying the neighborhood access to many sports fields, such as baseball fields and basketball courts.

St. Nicholas Park is another beautiful park that was built on the steeply banks of Central Harlem. This park features scenic hills, wide walkways two basketball courts, handball courts, picnic areas, chess tables, a large dog run, and three standard playgrounds.

For yet another park located in the Central Harlem area, Morningside Park spans 13 blocks and historically served as a divider between Morningside Heights and Harlem. The tree-lined paths are perfect for bike riding, jogging, or walking your dog, and tackling the stairs and hills is also a tough workout.

The abundance of park and recreational options in Central Harlem make choosing to live here a great choice.
